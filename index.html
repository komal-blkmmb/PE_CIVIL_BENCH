PE Civil Bench: Advancing Agentic Retrieval-Augmented Generation Performance of Large Language Models on Exam Problems and Automated Component Design

Saurav Silwal1, Joshua Gao2, Subin Varghese2, Vedhus Hoskere1,2*
1Dept. of Civil & Environmental Engineering, University of Houston 2Dept. of Electrical & Computer Engineering, University of Houston *Corresponding author: vhoskere@Central.UH.EDU

Published in Computer-Aided Civil and Infrastructure Engineering

Paper Code Data

Abstract

Large language models are increasingly being piloted in civil engineering firms to automate workflows and help engineers navigate dense design codes and specifications. However, safety-critical use remains limited by reliability concerns, inconsistent reasoning, and the need for auditable, code-grounded outputs suitable for quality control. While retrieval augmentation and agentic workflows have improved factuality in other domains, their effectiveness in civil engineering has not been systematically established, and it remains unclear how to scale them into transparent, code-compliant design pipelines with reliable evaluation.

To address these limitations, we develop the first benchmark, PE Civil Bench, consisting of Fundamentals of Engineering and Professional Engineering Civil examination questions and produce baseline scores of thirteen frontier and open-weight large language models, including the state-of-the-art GPT-5.5, under baseline prompting, conventional retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and agentic RAG. We then develop a retrieval-augmented multi-agent framework for fully automated concrete element design, demonstrated on reinforced concrete beams and columns, that infers design steps from worked examples and performs code retrieval, calculation, validation, and iterative self-correction with transparent traces. Across 33 beam configurations it produced code-compliant designs in all cases and closely matched ETABS finite-element outputs (r ≥ 0.90). Requiring only a new reference example and no reprogramming, the same architecture extended to column design. Finally, we introduce an autonomous RAG-based evaluator that extracts assessment criteria from reference solutions and agrees strongly with a traditional rule-based scorer (r = 0.976).

Three-Phase Methodology

From strategy benchmarking through automated design to quality verification: a unified pipeline for AI-assisted civil engineering.

Phase 1

Systematic Benchmarking

Three augmentation strategies of increasing sophistication (Base LLM, Vector RAG, and Agentic RAG) are evaluated on PE Civil Bench using LangChain with consistent hyperparameters across all 13 models.

Base LLM: intrinsic reasoning, no retrieval
Vector RAG: single-pass semantic retrieval from the FE Reference Handbook
Agentic RAG: iterative retrieval loop with formula injection and self-reflection (ReAct-inspired, LangGraph)
→
Phase 2

Agentic Design Generation

The best-performing strategy drives a self-correcting multi-agent framework for code-compliant structural design. Design logic is inferred dynamically: no hard-coded procedures.

Planner: extracts design steps from reference examples
Executor: agentic RAG retrieval + Python REPL for calculations
Validator: hierarchical error detection (local fix or full restart)
Report Generator: traceable, auditable output with ACI 318 references
→
Phase 3

Autonomous Evaluation

A RAG-based evaluation framework learns assessment criteria from reference solutions and scores designs without manual rubric construction.

Retriever: finds relevant reference designs by semantic search
Rubric Generator: extracts methodology and converts to scoring criteria
Evaluator: LLM-based agent assigns weighted scores with justifications

Evaluator Correlation: RAG-Based vs. Hardcoded Scoring

The autonomous RAG-based evaluator was validated against a traditional hardcoded scoring script across 66 RC beam designs (33 full-pipeline + 33 ablated without RAG). The near-perfect agreement confirms that retrieval-augmented evaluation can recover the expert judgment embedded in hand-crafted rubrics, without any manual rule construction. The Pearson correlation coefficient is r = 0.976, with R² = 0.953 indicating that 95.3% of variance in RAG-based scores is explained by the hardcoded scores. The RMSE of 4.69 represents under 8% of the 36–98 point scale, confirming practical accuracy.
